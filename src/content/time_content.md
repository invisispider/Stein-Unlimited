## A Brief History of Timekeeping

Humans have tracked time for thousands of years. Some of the earliest timekeeping devices were sundials used in ancient Egypt, which divided daylight into 12 segments. The Babylonians, operating on a base-60 system, gave us the 60-minute hour and 60-second minute.

The calendar we use today has roots in the Roman Empire. Julius Caesar’s Julian calendar introduced the 365-day year with a leap year every four years. Later, the Gregorian reform (1582) adjusted this to better align with the solar year.

Our 24-hour day traces back to ancient Egypt as well, but the mechanical clock—developed in Europe in the 14th century—made the division of time into precise hours, minutes, and seconds practical and widespread.

The modern week with seven days was adopted from Babylonian astrology, linked to the seven classical planets. Months are a relic of lunar tracking but were restructured under Roman emperors, giving us the uneven months and arbitrary names we use today.

These systems were designed for empires, churches, and agrarian societies — not for the digital, post-industrial, or self-directed life. unLimited Time rethinks this from the ground up.

### 🕔 What's wrong with my clock? 😦

The Roman system divide Earth's daily spin into **24 hours.** Each has **60 minutes**, each with **60 seconds.** Base-24 and base-60 wrap nicely around a circle and divide cleanly, but they make life harder for creatures who count on ten fingers and think in base-10.

### Days aren't round?

Early sundials traced the progress of a pole's shadow as the sun crossed the sky, resulting in circular depictions of the light half of a day. You might assume your clock's hour hand mirrors Earth's rotation, but it actually goes around twice per day, and the 12-hour circle has no meaning.

### A Scheduling Grid Clock

Pie charts are great for certain applications, like when you need to eyeball relative proportions. They are bad clocks. They're terrible for seeing **what happens when**. When we plan our day, we use lists and symmetrical grids — not three spinning pie charts (the clock hands.)

### A calendar measures history

We estimate the historical beginings of civilization
somewhere between 10 and 80+ thousand years ago (depending on what qualifies,) so we can start time anywhere

### Full compatibility

In common speech, we only use a year's last two digits

## Session
A **Session** is about how long an activity reasonably takes (about 2.7 hours), and is an amount of time that creates a noticable change in daylight.
We sleep through about three of them.
Sessions divide the day into 8 segments of the sun\'s journey, with one ~zen~ Session to reset the day and use up the extra time until Dawn (6am).

Events center around typical sleep and dietary habits, and often 
coincide with full activities including preparation and travel. A work period, an occasion, weather pattern, group interaction, or location trip are Events. Event names indicate a typical expression for that part of the day.

### Sessions and Events are intuitive.

Unlike with hours, we generally use
the name (eg. "Dawn") rather than the number. Resultantly, people will not even notice the difference when you speak Sessions. For great specificity, the Session can be
given as a decimal: 3\`50 would mean exactly halfway through Session 3, which is the fifth Event. This allows you to schedule all the way down to 100-Instant Whiles. For example, during your Morning Session, you might spend 3 Events at the gym, and you might switch exercize machines once in a While. People can only hold their breath for a Moment or two.

### 😎 It's Your time now.

While we present what people generally do in a day as a clock,
the point is not to prescribe a particular lifestyle or belief. 
This framework aims to encourage health and development, but is otherwise
unopinionated.

# Moments and Whiles{#moments}

## Units of situational awareness

### Whiles

People often sequence things 
in terms of roughly 15-18 minute periods, "an event". Being one minute early or late for something, even a Moment or a While, makes no real difference. An Event length of time means you entirely missed the Event of showing up.

### Moments

If you ask someone to describe what happened in a While, they
will describe a progression of "moments". Whiles and Moments serve mostly as 100-Instant containers. When we read the full clock time (or seconds into the day), we want to see it as a Session decimal followed by While decimal (Instants out of 100).
      
### Intuitive Language Units

The best way to test this system is to say things like, "I had a session.
It took a while to get there. I needed a moment to prepare," and ask yourself
whether you meant roughly what unLimited Time indicates.
